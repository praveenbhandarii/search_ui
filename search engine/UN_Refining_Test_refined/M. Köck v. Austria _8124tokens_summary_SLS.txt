Summary:

Case Details:
- Case name: M. Kck v. Austria
- Case ID: CRPD/C/26/D/50/2018
- Court/Tribunal: United Nations Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities
- Date of adoption of views: 18 May 2022
- Key parties involved: M. Kck (author), Austria (State party)

Procedural History:
- The author, a deaf individual who uses Austrian Sign Language (ASL), filed a complaint against Austria alleging violations of her rights under the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities.
- The State party provided observations on the admissibility and merits of the complaint.
- The case was heard by the United Nations Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities.

Legal Analysis and Reasoning:
- The author claimed violations of articles 5, 7, 12(3)(4), 13(1), 21(b) and (e), 24, and 30(4) of the Convention.
- The Committee interpreted article 5 of the Convention as requiring the State party to ensure equal access to education, including in Austrian Sign Language (ASL).
- The Committee found that the State party's failure to provide inclusive education in ASL amounted to discrimination, in violation of article 5 of the Convention.
- The State party's legislation and lack of curriculum for bilingual education were found to result in unequal treatment, breaching article 5 of the Convention.

Decisions and Rulings:
- The Committee found that the State party violated the author's rights under articles 5, 7, 12(3)(4), 13(1), 21(b) and (e), 24, and 30(4) of the Convention.
- The author was found to be unequal before and under the law, in breach of article 5 of the Convention.

Impact and Significance:
- The case highlights the importance of ensuring equal access to education for persons with disabilities, particularly those who are deaf or hard of hearing.
- The decision reinforces the obligation of States to provide inclusive education and to have legislation and policies in place to support this goal.
- The case also emphasizes the need for States to consider the best interests of the child and to protect and respect their rights.
