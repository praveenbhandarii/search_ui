Combined Summary:

Case Details:
- Case ID: CEDAW/C/74/D/104/2016
- Court/Tribunal: United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women (UNCEDAW)
- Document Date: 2019
- Key Parties Involved: Natalia Ciobanu (author) vs. Republic of Moldova
- Case Name: Natalia Ciobanu v. Republic of Moldova
- Case Summary: Natalia Ciobanu claims that the Republic of Moldova violated her rights under article 3 and article 11 (2) (c) of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women (CEDAW) by not including the period of care for her disabled child in the calculation of her social insurance pension contribution period.

Procedural History:
- The author, Natalia Ciobanu, submitted communication No. 104/2016 on 3 May 2016, alleging violation of rights under articles 3 and 11(2)(c) of the Convention.
- The Optional Protocol entered into force for the Republic of Moldova on 31 May 2006.
- The author is not represented by counsel.
- The author exhausted all available and effective domestic remedies.

Legal Analysis and Reasoning:
- The author claims that the State party did not ensure the existence of a legal framework that would contribute to the social and economic development of women who have children with severe disabilities in their care.
- The State party's failure to provide alternatives to institutionalization for children with disabilities and the introduction of the social service of personal assistant in 2012 are key contextual factors.

Decisions and Rulings:
- The Committee found that the Republic of Moldova violated the author's rights under article 3 and article 11 (2) (c) of CEDAW.
- The State party was requested to recalculate the author's social insurance pension, provide adequate compensation, and ensure the availability of adequate support services for mothers of severely disabled children.

Impact and Significance:
- The case sets a precedent for recognizing the rights of women providing care for children with disabilities and the need for social security schemes to be non-discriminatory.
- It highlights the intersectionality of gender and disability discrimination and the importance of addressing the economic vulnerability of caregivers.
