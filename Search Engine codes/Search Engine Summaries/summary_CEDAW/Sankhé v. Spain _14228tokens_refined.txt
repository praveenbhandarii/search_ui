Combined Summary:

Case Details:
- Case ID: CEDAW/C/56/D/29/2011
- Court/Tribunal: United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women (UNCEDAW)
- Document Dates: Decision adopted by the Committee at its 56th session, 30 September-18 October 2013
- Key Parties Involved:
  - Party A: Mamouna Sankh (alleged victim)
  - Party B: Spain (state party)
- Case Name: Mamouna Sankh v. Spain
- Case Summary: Mamouna Sankh, a Senegalese national, alleges violations by Spain of articles 1, 2(a)(g), 3, 6, 9(1), 10(a), 11(1)(a) and (d), 11(2)(c), 15(1) and (2), and 16(1)(c), (d) and (f) of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women.

Procedural History:
- The author, Mamouna Sankh, arrived in Spain in 2000 and later married a Nigerian citizen with permanent legal residency in Spain.
- The author's temporary residence and work permit was denied, leading to an administrative appeal with the Ministry of Labour and Immigration.
- The Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women requested Spain to refrain from deporting the author while her case was being considered.

Legal Analysis and Reasoning:
- The legal issues addressed include the admissibility of the communication, alleged violations of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women, employment and residence permit issues, and deportation proceedings.
- Relevant legal provisions include various articles of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women.
- The Court's analysis focused on the author's claims of discrimination against women, specifically with regards to marriage, employment, and residency permits.

Decisions and Rulings:
- The Committee found that the communication was inadmissible under article 4(1) of the Optional Protocol, as domestic remedies had not been exhausted and the failure to exhaust such remedies was not attributable to the State party.

Impact and Significance:
- The decision sets a precedent for the exhaustion of domestic remedies in cases of alleged violations of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women.
- It highlights the importance of following proper legal procedures and exhausting all available domestic remedies before seeking relief under international conventions.
