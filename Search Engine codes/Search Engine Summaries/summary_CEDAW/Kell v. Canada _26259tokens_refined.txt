- Court/Tribunal: United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women (UNCEDAW)
- Document Dates: Year 2012
- Key Parties Involved:
  - Cecilia Kell (alleged victim)
  - Canada (state party)
- Case Name: Cecilia Kell v. Canada

Procedural History:
The United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women (CEDAW) considered Communication No. 19/2008, submitted by Cecilia Kell, a Canadian aboriginal woman. The communication alleged violations by Canada of Kell's rights under the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women (CEDAW).

Legal Analysis and Reasoning:
The legal details of the case include the articles of the Covenant under which the alleged violations occurred, the procedural and substantive issues, and the subject matter. The communication alleged discrimination against women in accessing housing and spousal abuse, and the State party challenged the admissibility of the communication on various grounds.

Decisions and Rulings:
The Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women found the communication admissible and concluded that the author's allegations were sufficiently substantiated for the purposes of admissibility. The Committee also noted that the author's claim had not been barred by limitation and that her claim constituted a pending claim.

Impact and Significance:
The Committee's decision to find the communication admissible sets a precedent for addressing discrimination against women in accessing housing and spousal abuse. It also highlights the importance of ensuring effective legal protection for victims of discrimination and violence.
