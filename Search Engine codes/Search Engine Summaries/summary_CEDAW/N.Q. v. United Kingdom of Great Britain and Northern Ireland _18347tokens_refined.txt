Combined Summary:

Case Details:
- Case ID: CEDAW/C/63/D/62/2013
- Court/Tribunal: United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women (UNCEDAW)
- Document Dates: 2016
- Key Parties Involved:
  - N.Q. (alleged victim) and S.A. (her husband) vs. United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland
- Case Name: N.Q. and S.A. v. United Kingdom of Great Britain and Northern Ireland
- Case Summary: The authors, N.Q. and S.A., both Pakistani nationals, submitted a communication to the UNCEDAW claiming that their rights under the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women would be violated if they were deported to Pakistan, where their lives would be at risk. The State party, the United Kingdom, deported the authors on October 1, 2013, despite the Committees request to refrain from doing so while the communication was under consideration.

Procedural History:
- Date of communication: September 24, 2013 (initial submission)
- Decision of admissibility: March 2, 2015
- Date of adoption of views: February 25, 2016

Legal Analysis and Reasoning:
- Article of the Covenant: Article 7 (3) of the Optional Protocol to the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women
- Submitted by: N.Q. and S.A.
- Procedural issue: Admissibility of the communication
- Substantive issue: Violation of rights under the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women
- Subject matter: Deportation of the authors to Pakistan

Decisions and Rulings:
- The Committee declared the communication admissible and requested the State party to refrain from expelling the authors while the communication was under consideration.
- In its present views, the Committee found that the authors had not exhausted domestic remedies and that the communication was manifestly ill-founded and/or not sufficiently substantiated. Therefore, the Committee declared the communication inadmissible pursuant to article 4 (2) (c) of the Optional Protocol.

Impact and Significance:
- The case sets a precedent for the consideration of gender-based persecution in asylum claims and the obligation of states to protect individuals from such persecution.
