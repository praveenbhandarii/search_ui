Case Details:
- Case ID: CEDAW/C/80/D/145/2019
- Court/Tribunal: United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women (UNCEDAW)
- Document Dates: 2021
- Key Parties Involved:
  - Party A: M.A. (the author and victim, represented by counsel, Leila Boussemacer, of the Protestant Social Centre in Geneva)
  - Party B: Switzerland
Case Name: M.A. v. Switzerland
Case Summary:
M.A., an Eritrean national, applied for asylum in Switzerland, which ordered her expulsion to France pursuant to the Dublin III Regulation of the European Union. M.A. claims that her expulsion would violate her rights under articles 2 (d) and 6 of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women.

Procedural History:
The communication was initially submitted on June 26, 2019. The decision was adopted on November 1, 2021. M.A. was represented by counsel, Leila Boussemacer, of the Protestant Social Centre in Geneva. The jurisdiction and competence lie with the United Nations Committee on the Elimination of Discrimination against Women.

Legal Analysis and Reasoning:
The legal issues addressed include the violation of articles 2 (d) and 6 of the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women. The Court analyzed the substantive issue of whether the expulsion of M.A. to France would violate her rights under the Convention, as well as the procedural issue of the request for suspensive effect of the expulsion order pending consideration by the Committee.

Decisions and Rulings:
The Committee found that M.A.'s rights had been violated and requested Switzerland to refrain from expelling her while her communication was under consideration. The Committee also requested Switzerland to provide information on the measures taken to ensure that M.A.'s rights under the Convention were protected.

Impact and Significance:
This case sets a precedent for the protection of the rights of female asylum seekers under the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination against Women. It highlights the importance of considering the specific vulnerabilities of women in asylum procedures and the potential risks they face in the process.
