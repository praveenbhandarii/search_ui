Title: Whaling in the Antarctic (Australia v. Japan: New Zealand intervening)

Case Overview:
The case of Whaling in the Antarctic (Australia v. Japan: New Zealand intervening) was brought before the International Court of Justice (ICJ) in 2010. Australia alleged that Japan's whaling program, known as JARPA II, violated its obligations under the International Convention for the Regulation of Whaling (ICRW) and other international obligations for the preservation of marine mammals and the marine environment.

Document Overview:
This document is a judgment type document from the ICJ in Case Number 148. It provides important information regarding the regulation of whaling and the role of the International Whaling Commission (IWC). The document includes details about the case, such as the judge, document date, and case number. It also cites the International Convention for the Regulation of Whaling (ICRW) and other relevant resolutions.

Important Details:
- The ICJ ruled that Japan's scientific whaling program, JARPA II, is not justified as scientific research and is not the main purpose of the program.
- Japan's use of lethal methods in JARPA II is not justified under Article VIII of the ICRW.
- The special permits granted by Japan in connection with JARPA II do not fall under Article VIII (1) of the ICRW.
- Japan's actions are in breach of Article VIII(1) of the ICRW.
- The ICJ emphasized the importance of nonlethal research methods and the need to replace lethal methods with nonlethal ones whenever possible.
- The ICJ referred to several resolutions issued by the IWC and the Scientific Committee concerning JARPA II and its use of lethal methods.
- The judgment sets a precedent for the interpretation and application of the ICRW in the future.
- The case has contributed to the gradual formation of an opinio juris communis in the domain of conservation and sustainable use of living marine resources.
- The primacy of the jus necessarium over the jus voluntarium is increasingly recognized in international law, particularly in the era of international tribunals.

- The ICJ's judgment highlights the importance of the precautionary approach and the principle of prevention in the interpretation and implementation of the ICRW.
