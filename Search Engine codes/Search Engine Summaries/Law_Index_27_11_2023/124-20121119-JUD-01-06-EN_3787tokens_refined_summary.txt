Title: Territorial and Maritime Dispute (Nicaragua v. Colombia)

Case Overview:
This case, numbered 124, involves a territorial and maritime dispute between Nicaragua and Colombia. Nicaragua filed an application in 2001, initiating proceedings against Colombia regarding legal issues related to territory and maritime delimitation. The proceedings were suspended in 2003 due to Colombia's preliminary objections to jurisdiction.

Important Details:
- Nicaragua argues for its right to a continental shelf beyond 200 nautical miles, based on customary international law, even though it is not a party to UNCLOS.
- The International Court of Justice (ICJ) determines that Nicaragua's evidence is inadequate to delimit a continental shelf beyond 200 nautical miles.
- The ICJ states that the court lacks sufficient basis to accede to Nicaragua's delimitation request.
- UNCLOS Article 76, paragraphs 8 and 9, and customary international law are relevant provisions.
- The ICJ recognizes that non-UNCLOS states may assert a continental shelf beyond 200 nautical miles if the necessary conditions exist.
- States parties to UNCLOS have obligations to establish final and binding outer limits of their continental shelves beyond 200 nautical miles.
- The ICJ emphasizes that UNCLOS does not impose obligations on states that have chosen not to be bound by the convention.
- Nicaragua's evidence is deemed inadequate for delimiting a continental shelf beyond 200 nautical miles.

Document Details:
This document is a judgment delivered by the ICJ on 12 July 2018. Judge Mensah presided over the case. The document is found in ICJ Reports 2018, pages 762-763.

Additional Information:
- Another case, numbered CIJ1034, involves a territorial and maritime dispute between Nicaragua and Honduras. The judgment was delivered on December 14, 2012.
- Judge Thomas A. Mensah expresses concerns about the implications of the judgment and the potential impact on third states.
- Bilateral agreements concluded in the area are considered an informal multilateral framework for managing the disputes.
- The document cites the significance of these agreements on the public order of the oceans.
- Judge Mensah questions whether relying solely on Article 59 of the Court's Statute would adequately protect the rights of third states.
