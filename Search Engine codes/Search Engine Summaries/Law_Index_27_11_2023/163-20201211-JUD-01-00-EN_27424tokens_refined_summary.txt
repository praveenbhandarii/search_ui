Title: Immunities and Criminal Proceedings (Equatorial Guinea v. France)

Case Overview:
The case involves a dispute between Equatorial Guinea and France regarding the immunity from criminal jurisdiction of the Second Vice-President of Equatorial Guinea and the legal status of the building housing the Embassy of Equatorial Guinea in France. Equatorial Guinea alleged that France violated its international obligations under the United Nations Convention against Transnational Organized Crime and general international law.

Document Overview:
This judgment document from the International Court of Justice (ICJ) provides details of the case, including the case name, case number, date, court, and judges involved. It also highlights the key issues, findings, decision, legal grounds, requested relief, and various documents related to the case.

Important Details:
- Equatorial Guinea filed an application with the ICJ against France, claiming a breach of international law for initiating criminal proceedings against the Vice-President of Equatorial Guinea and attaching a building belonging to Equatorial Guinea's diplomatic mission in Paris.
- France objected to Equatorial Guinea's designation of the building as premises of its diplomatic mission, arguing that it was timely and based on reasonable grounds.
- The ICJ held that France's objection was not arbitrary or discriminatory and that the building never acquired the status of premises of the mission under the Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations.
- Equatorial Guinea based its claim on Article 35 of the United Nations Convention against Transnational Organized Crime and Article I of the Optional Protocol to the Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations.
- The ICJ awarded no relief to Equatorial Guinea and rejected its claims.
- The case involved oral arguments presented by the parties during the hearings.

Additional Information:
- The case was heard by the ICJ in 2018, and a judgment on preliminary objections was rendered on June 6, 2018.
- France made counterclaims against Equatorial Guinea, challenging the jurisdiction of the court and the alleged harm or violation of obligations by Equatorial Guinea.
- The case also involved a separate legal proceeding in the French Tribunal de grande instance, where searches were conducted at the building in question, and the Embassy of Equatorial Guinea claimed that it was part of the diplomatic mission and protected under the Vienna Convention.
- The Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations, French law, and relevant judicial decisions were cited in the case.
- The International Court of Justice ordered that any confiscation measure against the building in Paris could not be executed until the conclusion of the international judicial proceedings.
- The case raises important issues regarding the interpretation of the Vienna Convention, the consent of the receiving state for designating premises of a diplomatic mission, and the relationship between sending and receiving states.
- The case also involves a meeting between the French Ministry of Foreign Affairs and the Ambassador of Equatorial Guinea on February 16, 2012, and an interim order issued by the Tribunal de grande instance of Paris on October 22, 2013.
