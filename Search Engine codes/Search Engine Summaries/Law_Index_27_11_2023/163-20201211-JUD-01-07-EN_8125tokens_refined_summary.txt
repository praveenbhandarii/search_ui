Title: Immunities and Criminal Proceedings (Equatorial Guinea v. France)

Case Overview:
The case (Case Number 163) involves a dispute between the Republic of Equatorial Guinea and France regarding the immunity from criminal jurisdiction of the Second Vice-President of Equatorial Guinea and the legal status of the building housing the Embassy of Equatorial Guinea in France. The proceedings were initiated by Equatorial Guinea on June 13, 2016.

Document Overview:
This judgment document, dated January 22, 2022, pertains to the case between Equatorial Guinea and France. It includes dissenting opinions from Judge Ad Hoc Kateka and Judge ad hoc Mensah. The document references specific paragraphs (38 and 1418) of the judgment and cites the Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (VCDR) and other relevant legal provisions.

Important Details:

- Judge Kateka dissented, arguing that the VCDR does require prior consent from the receiving State for the designation of diplomatic premises.
- The majority's interpretation of the VCDR emphasizes mutual rights and obligations between sending and receiving States, with the receiving State having no power to object without a valid reason.
- The dissenting opinion highlights the importance of consultation and mutual agreement in the designation of diplomatic premises.
- The case revolves around the recognition of a building in Paris as part of Equatorial Guinea's diplomatic mission, with France refusing to acknowledge its status.
- Key issues include the entitlement to immunity under the VCDR, France's refusal to recognize the building, and the interpretation of relevant VCDR provisions.
- The majority's approach is criticized for ignoring the use condition in the VCDR and failing to provide a clear rule for determining the status of diplomatic premises.
