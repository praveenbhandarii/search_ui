Summary:

Case Details:
- Case Name: Lauren Henley (represented by counsel, Erin Turner Manners) v. The State Party (Australia)
- Case ID: CRPD/C/27/D/56/2018
- Court/Tribunal: United Nations Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities
- Document Details: Views adopted by the Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities under article 5 of the Optional Protocol to the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities
- Document Dates: 3 October 2018 (initial submission), 28 November 2018 (not issued in document form), 15 February 2023 (Committee's views adopted)
- Key Parties Involved: Lauren Henley (author), The State Party (Australia)

Procedural History:
- The author, who is completely blind, submitted a complaint to the Australian Human Rights Commission regarding the lack of audio description on freeto-air television.
- The Commission discontinued the investigation, and the author applied for an administrative review, which was dismissed.
- The author then submitted a communication to the UNCRPD, alleging violations of her rights under the Convention.

Legal Analysis and Reasoning:
- The author alleges that the State party has violated her rights under the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities by failing to provide audio description on freeto-air television, in violation of articles 9(1)(b) and 30(1)(b) of the CRPD.
- The Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities found that the State party has failed to provide audio description on freeto-air television, preventing accessibility for persons with visual impairments.
- The Committee also found that the State party has failed to substantiate its claims that several measures have been taken to address the issue of audio description on television.
- The Committee further found that the author has exhausted available domestic remedies and that her complaint is admissible.

Decisions and Rulings:
- The Committee adopted Views in accordance with article 5 of the Optional Protocol, concerning communication No. 56/2018.
- The Committee found that the State party has violated the author's rights under articles 9(1)(b) and 30(1)(b) of the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities.

Impact and Significance:
- This case highlights the importance of ensuring accessibility for persons with disabilities, particularly those with visual impairments, in the context of freeto-air television.
- The decision of the Committee on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities sets a precedent for other countries to follow in ensuring accessibility for persons with disabilities in the context of television programming.
- The case also emphasizes the importance of exhausting domestic remedies before bringing a complaint to an international human rights body.
