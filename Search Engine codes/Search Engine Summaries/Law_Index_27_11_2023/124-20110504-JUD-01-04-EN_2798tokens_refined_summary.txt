Title: Territorial and Maritime Dispute (Nicaragua v. Colombia)

Case Overview:
The case involves a territorial and maritime dispute between Nicaragua and Colombia in the Caribbean Sea. Nicaragua filed an application in 2001, and Colombia raised preliminary objections to jurisdiction in 2003, leading to the suspension of proceedings on the merits.

Important Details:
- Honduras applied for permission to intervene in the case but was denied by the ICJ.
- The ICJ found that Honduras did not demonstrate a legal interest affected by the case and dismissed their arguments regarding the 1986 Treaty.
- The Court distinguished between rights and legal interests of third States seeking to intervene.
- The consent of the parties to the main case is not required for the consideration of requests for permission to intervene.
- The Court's jurisdiction to consider intervention applications is derived from the original consent given by parties to the Court's Statute.

Document Details:
This judgment, dated June 14, 2013, is a joint declaration by Judges Antonio Augusto Canado Trindade and Abdulqawi Ahmed Yusuf regarding the ICJ's jurisdiction to consider applications for permission to intervene. It emphasizes that the consent of the parties to the main case is not a condition for intervention as a nonparty under Article 62 of the ICJ Statute. The document cites previous cases to support the Court's jurisdiction and is part of the ICJ Reports publication.

Additional Information:
The document clarifies the ICJ judges' positions on the relevance of state consent in considering intervention applications under Article 62 of the ICJ Statute. It highlights that the Court's jurisdiction is not dependent on the consent of the parties to the main case. The judges' statement is signed by both judges and provides important insights into the ICJ's approach to intervention cases.
