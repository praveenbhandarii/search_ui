Title: Immunities and Criminal Proceedings (Equatorial Guinea v. France)

Case Overview:
This case involves a dispute between the Republic of Equatorial Guinea and the French Republic regarding the immunity from criminal jurisdiction of the Second Vice-President of Equatorial Guinea, Mr. Teodoro Nguema Obiang Mangue, and the legal status of the building housing the Embassy of Equatorial Guinea in France.

Document Overview:
This judgment document, issued by the International Court of Justice (ICJ) on 6 June 2018, provides a summary of the case and its key issues. It also addresses the preliminary objections raised by France and the court's decision on jurisdiction. The document includes relevant dates, court details, and case numbers.

Important Details:
- Equatorial Guinea filed an application against France on 13 June 2016, alleging violations of the Second Vice-President's immunity and the inviolability of the embassy building.
- France raised three preliminary objections, challenging the ICJ's jurisdiction under the Palermo Convention and the Optional Protocol to the Vienna Convention, as well as alleging abuse of process and rights.
- The ICJ rejected all three preliminary objections, asserting its jurisdiction to hear the case.
- The court held that Article 4 of the Palermo Convention does not incorporate customary international rules on immunities of States and State officials.
- France was found not to have breached its obligations under the Convention.
- The building at 42 Avenue Foch in Paris was deemed not immune from measures of constraint as State property.
- The ICJ's operative clause confirms the rejection of the preliminary objections and the court's jurisdiction to hear the case.
- Mr. Teodoro Nguema Obiang Mangue appeared for a hearing on the merits on 2 January 2017, and the hearings on the merits of the case began on 19 June 2017.
- On 27 October 2017, the tribunal delivered its judgment, finding Mr. Obiang guilty of money laundering offenses and sentencing him to a three-year suspended sentence.
- The ICJ ruled on the preliminary objection on 29 March 2019, finding that the Palermo Convention does not confer exclusive jurisdiction on Equatorial Guinea over the Vice President and the building.
- The ICJ found that France has not violated the Palermo Convention by asserting jurisdiction over the Vice President and the building.
- Equatorial Guinea's claims include the recognition of the embassy building as State property and France's alleged violation of inviolability and immunity under the Vienna Convention.
